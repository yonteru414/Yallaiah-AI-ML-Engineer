Step 3: Covariance Matrix

Measuring how features vary together

The covariance matrix quantifies how features change together. The formula for covariance between features i and j is:

Covij = (1/(n-1)) * Σk=1n (xk,i - μi)(xk,j - μj), where n=4

For example, the covariance between Income and Credit Utilization is calculated as:

[(-1375)(-20) + (625)(20) + (-1875)(30) + (2625)(-30)] / 3 = -31,666.67

Step 4: Correlation Matrix

Standardizing the relationships between features

The correlation matrix standardizes the covariance values to a range of -1 to 1, making it easier to interpret the strength of relationships between features. The formula is:

Corrij = Covij / √(Vari * Varj)

Step 5: Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) decomposition

PCA involves finding the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the covariance matrix. Eigenvalues represent the amount of variance captured by each principal component, while eigenvectors represent the direction of these components.

Eigenvalue Calculation

The eigenvalues are calculated by solving the characteristic equation det(Cov - λI) = 0, where I is the identity matrix. For our covariance matrix, the eigenvalues are:

Rank Analysis

The rank of the covariance matrix is 3, indicating that only 3 of the 5 features are linearly independent. This suggests that 2 features are redundant and can be expressed as linear combinations of the others.

Step 7: Comprehensive Analysis

Connecting mathematical results to customer profiles

Mathematical Interpretation of Customer Behavior

Ramu's Negative Covariance Pattern

Ramu exhibits a classic negative covariance pattern between income and credit utilization. Despite having below-average income (-$1,375 from mean), Ramu maintains a healthy utilization rate (30%, which is 20% below mean). This negative relationship is mathematically captured by the covariance value of -31,666.67 between Income and Utilization.

Ramu's centered data shows:

  • Income: -1,375 (below mean)
  • Utilization: -20 (below mean)
  • Payment Delay: -4.75 (below mean)
  • Transactions: +7.5 (above mean)
  • Credit Score: +47.5 (above mean)

This pattern demonstrates that Ramu manages credit responsibly despite limited income, resulting in a strong credit score (720). The negative covariance between income and utilization is evident as Ramu's lower income correlates with lower utilization.

Somu's Positive Covariance Pattern

Somu represents the opposite pattern with strong positive covariance between risk factors. With the lowest income (-$1,875 from mean), Somu shows the highest utilization (+30 from mean) and most payment delays (+8.25 from mean). This positive relationship is reflected in the covariance matrix.

Somu's centered data shows:

  • Income: -1,875 (lowest)
  • Utilization: +30 (highest)
  • Payment Delay: +8.25 (highest)
  • Transactions: -22.5 (lowest)
  • Credit Score: -92.5 (lowest)

Somu's data perfectly illustrates the strong positive covariance between utilization and payment delay (203.33), and the negative covariance between these factors and credit score. This combination results in the highest risk profile.

PCA Interpretation of Customer Differences

The PCA results reveal that 99.89% of the variance in customer behavior is captured by PC1, which is dominated by income. This explains why Gopu (highest income) and Somu (lowest income) represent the extremes in our dataset.

PC2, though explaining only 0.11% of variance, captures the credit behavior dimension. Here we see:

  • Ramu and Gopu have negative PC2 scores (responsible credit behavior)
  • Raghu and Somu have positive PC2 scores (risky credit behavior)

This mathematical decomposition aligns perfectly with our risk assessment, showing that while income dominates the variance, credit behavior patterns are still discernible in the secondary component.

Covariance Matrix Insights

The covariance matrix provides several key insights about our customer dataset:

Income-Utilization Relationship (-31,666.67): This negative covariance shows that customers with lower income (like Ramu and Somu) tend to have higher credit utilization, while higher income customers (like Raghu and Gopu) use less of their available credit. This is a key indicator of financial stress in lower-income customers.
Utilization-Delay Relationship (203.33): This positive covariance indicates that customers who use more of their credit (like Raghu and Somu) are more likely to delay payments. This pattern is particularly strong in Somu's case, who has both the highest utilization and most delays.
Delay-Score Relationship (-539.17): This negative covariance demonstrates the severe impact of payment delays on credit scores. Somu's 15 delays correlate with the lowest score (580), while Gopu's zero delays correlate with the highest score (750).

Mathematical Confirmation of Risk Assessment

Our risk assessment is mathematically confirmed by the eigenvalue decomposition. The fact that PC1 captures 99.89% of variance (primarily income) explains why:

  • Gopu (highest income) is classified as very low risk
  • Somu (lowest income) is classified as highest risk

However, the secondary component (PC2) reveals that income alone doesn't tell the whole story. Ramu, despite lower income, demonstrates responsible behavior (negative PC2 score), resulting in a low-risk classification. Conversely, Raghu, despite higher income, shows risky behavior (positive PC2 score), resulting in a medium-high risk classification.

Key Mathematical Takeaways:

  • The covariance matrix quantifies the relationships between financial behaviors, showing clear patterns of risk factors
  • PCA decomposition reveals that income dominates the variance (99.89%), but credit behavior patterns are still discernible
  • The rank of 3 indicates that only 3 independent factors drive customer behavior, with 2 features being redundant
  • Customer risk profiles align perfectly with the mathematical patterns observed in covariance and PCA results

Step 8: Key Takeaways

Summary of insights from the PCA analysis

Technical Insights

  • Covariance Matrix: Shows absolute scale relationships, with Income dominating due to its large variance
  • Correlation Matrix: Reveals strong behavioral links between credit utilization, payment delays, and credit scores
  • PCA Results: Identifies 3 independent factors in the data, with 2 redundant features
  • Dominant Component: PC1 captures 99.89% of variance, primarily driven by Income

Business Insights

  • Risk Indicators: Payment delays and high credit utilization are the strongest predictors of credit risk
  • Income Impact: Higher income correlates with better credit behavior but doesn't guarantee low risk
  • Customer Segmentation: Clear risk profiles emerge: low risk (Ramu, Gopu), medium-high risk (Raghu), and highest risk (Somu)
  • Feature Redundancy: Payment delays and credit scores are nearly perfectly correlated, suggesting one could potentially be dropped

Recommendations:

  • Focus monitoring on customers with high credit utilization and payment delays
  • Consider developing targeted interventions for medium-risk customers like Raghu to prevent escalation
  • For future modeling, consider standardizing features to reduce income's dominance in PCA
  • Explore if transaction volume can be incentivized for higher-risk customers to potentially improve credit scores
